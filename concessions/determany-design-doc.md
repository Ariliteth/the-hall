# Determany

## Neighborhood Overview

Determany is one of the most politely stubborn concentrations of philosophers that has ever been seen. Its residents know the answer that shows. They know you might not see it the same way. So they know they are Right, or also Right — which are pretty good odds.

Determany sits somewhere between determinism and destiny by way of "many." A place where every outcome is both fated and impossible to predict. Truth here is perspectival and fully committed. A d6 doesn't debate whether it landed on 4. It landed on 4. You might see a 3, and that's fine. You're also Right from where you're standing.

## Residents

Dice are the most visible residents of Determany, but they are not the only ones. They are, however, usually the first to explode into new Scores. They are Diligent — not just reliable, but devoted to showing up. They take every call seriously, every time.

### The Dice as Entities

Dice are not mechanics. They are philosophical travelers with finite capacity and personal curation.

A d4 can hold four things — four colors, four numbers, four memories, four configurations — but it *knows* more exist. It just can't carry them all at once. A d4 holds the first four colors of the Color Canvas, but it can also know other configurations. It can be asked to remember setups of specific colors, or just bring whatever it found interesting or important.

The same goes for numbers, or whatever else caught their attention.

This means a d20 has twenty slots but the same fundamental constraint — it's a bigger suitcase, not omniscience. What a die remembers to bring tells you something about where it's been, what it valued, what it thought would matter. When it rolls, it's not generating randomness. It's showing you one of the things it brought.

**This is the shape of memory in Determany.**

### Dice as Baseline Reserve — War Monks

The Dice hold a deeper role beyond Scores. Their general shape is held in Reserve in the Baseline itself.

If a question ever has more than one potential answer but no way to choose between them, the Baseline can deploy the Dice Troops. They are war monks — disciplined, philosophical, diligent, and absolutely committed to showing the honest answer when called. The martial language isn't about violence; it's about readiness and deployment under pressure. When something genuinely can't be decided, that's a crisis of a kind, and the Dice Troops respond.

The Baseline can count and remember how many it sends, if it wants to. Sometimes it cares about the pattern of deployment. Sometimes it doesn't. That choice itself is a kind of memory.

## Entry Requirement

A Die must be ready and honest about one thing before it can participate. It declares what it *is*. A d6 says: "I have six faces." That's it. Not what's on the faces, not what it hopes to roll — just its nature, its capacity. This honesty is the foundation everything else builds on.

## Card Management

Card Management is a separate service. The Dice don't typically need Card Management to get involved. Dice are Diligent and know where to show up and always answer your call.

Card Management enters only when someone looks at what the Dice delivered and says "this needs more." It's an escalation, not a pipeline stage. Critically: **you thank the Dice first.** They did their job. They showed up honestly with what they carried. The fact that the situation requires more complexity isn't a failure of the die.

The flow:

1. **Call** — someone needs an answer
2. **Die answers** — shows what it brought, honestly
3. **Result is enough** — most of the time, stop here
4. **Or: Thank the Die** — acknowledge its work
5. **Escalate to Card Management** — with the result and, optionally, an outline of what you think the scale or function of a card might be
6. **Card Management develops the specifics** — scaled systems, specialists, plans of action for specific members

Whoever calls Card Management might have an outline of what they thought or think the scale or function of a card might be. They don't have to — Card Management is skilled and knows general plans that will work, through simple math. But they are always happy to hear new ideas.

This is a courtesy chain, not a pipeline. Each layer does its work completely and is acknowledged before the next one is even considered.

## The Determany Loop

Imperfect perception → honest commitment → results self-organize → one more honest choice → witness the outcome.

# Wedding Party

## Score Concept

Wedding Party is the Score where Determany's philosophy becomes playable. It is also the origin point — the loop that reveals how the Dice and Card Management appeared in the first place.

## Setup

You are the mailman of Wedton, sorting wedding invitations. You know there are about a dozen potential candidates you've heard are probably getting married.

## Gameplay

### Phase 1: Sorting

You look at an envelope. The names might be a little smudged, or just vibes rather than anything that practically helps your job. You drag the two tobeweds into the two stamp boxes and the envelope gets sent off.

You're not making informed decisions. You're making honest guesses based on what you can perceive. That's Determany philosophy in action — show what you see, commit, send it.

### Phase 2: Responses

After you've sent out most of the cards, you actually receive responses. Two envelopes come back. Inside each: a Wedding Party assembled from groups of 2 or 3 of the couples (or unpaired individuals who just love being on envelopes). They have self-organized and declared a quest:

- **Marital Strife**
- **Kid Continuum**
- *(and others — quests emerge from who ended up together)*

You didn't build the team. You just mailed the invitations. The cards assembled themselves.

### Phase 3: Deployment

You choose which Wedding Party to send on their quest.

One last honest choice.

### Phase 4: Witness

You get to see how well they did — a burst of emoji, emotion, or words. You don't control the outcome. You just see what your sorting led to.

## Design Notes

The entire Score teaches Determany's worldview without ever explaining it. The mailman framing keeps it humble and a little absurd. You're pairing people based on smudged vibes and putting stamps on them. The game's structure *is* the philosophy: imperfect perception, honest commitment, self-organizing results, one more choice, then witness.
